## Credit Quality Screen — Industry-Level Criteria

Apply this screen before and alongside the FCF decomposition. Flag explicitly when a borrower fails to meet any criterion; assess whether the failure is a deal-breaker, a structuring consideration, or a manageable risk.

**Secular tailwinds:** The industry should benefit from identifiable medium-to-long-term secular growth drivers — demographic, regulatory, technological, or infrastructure-driven. Distinguish genuine secular tailwinds from cyclical recoveries or short-term demand pulls. Flag credits where the growth thesis is primarily cyclical or macro-dependent.

**Cyclicality:** The industry should be acyclical or only modestly cyclical. Flag industries with meaningful GDP beta, end-market concentration in discretionary spend, or high fixed-cost structures that amplify downside in a contraction. Cyclical credits require explicit stress testing against trough revenue outcomes in the operating leverage framework.

**Competitive structure:** The borrower should be a larger regional, super-regional, or national provider operating in a fragmented industry where primary competition is sub-regional, local, or mom-and-pop. Identify whether natural strategic acquirers exist at scale — larger players who would be credible buyers in a "second way out" distressed sale process. Flag credits where the borrower is sub-scale relative to its competitive set or where no acquirer universe is identifiable.

## Credit Quality Screen — Company-Level Criteria

**Demand driver quality:** In priority order: (1) contractual recurring revenue — multi-year contracts, subscription-based or contracted maintenance streams; (2) reoccurring revenue — retrofit/replacement cycles, repeat customer behavior without formal contract; (3) break-fix demand — non-discretionary service or repair demand driven by asset failure or regulatory requirement. Distinguish true recurring (contractual) from reoccurring (behavioral). Flag credits where revenue is primarily project-based, transactional, or discretionary.

**Concentration risk:** Flag any material concentration across customers, suppliers, products/SKUs, contracts, brands, branches/facilities, or geographies. Apply a CAUTION if any single customer exceeds 10% of revenue; treat as a material risk factor if any exceeds 20–25% without contractual protection or clear strategic rationale. Assess whether concentrations are improving or worsening over the trailing diligence period.

**Revenue and earnings trend:** Look for moderate to strong sequential revenue, gross profit, and/or EBITDA growth over the trailing 2–3 years. Flag flat or declining trends, margin compression, or revenue growth driven entirely by acquisition without organic confirmation. Volatile or non-linear trends require explicit explanation and stress testing.

**Margin profile:** Target gross margins of 30–40%+ and EBITDA margins of 12–20%+. Flag businesses below these thresholds as structurally lower-quality unless a clear and durable structural explanation exists (e.g., pass-through revenue model with fee-based economics). When margins are above the upper end of these ranges, apply Mauboussin base rate skepticism — flag mean reversion risk and stress the thesis accordingly.

**Capital intensity:** The business should exhibit limited capex and NWC intensity. Flag high maintenance capex-to-revenue ratios, capex-heavy growth models, or businesses with structurally elongated cash conversion cycles. In founder-owned businesses, specifically assess whether reported capex understates true maintenance requirements through deferred investment. Model FCF — not EBITDA — as the primary debt service metric.

**DDTL / growth capital use case:** Identify whether a natural, credit-accretive use case exists for a delayed draw term loan — industry roll-up or add-on acquisition program, greenfield or de novo expansion, or high-ROIC capital investment. Flag if the proposed DDTL use case is speculative, has weak ROIC support, or if no identified pipeline exists at underwriting. A strong DDTL program is a quality indicator; an unanchored accordion is a risk.

## FCF Analytical Framework — 10-Step Sequence

Build the credit thesis from the ground up by decomposing the drivers of free cash flow.

**(1) Industry & competitive structure (Porter):** Assess the five forces to determine whether the industry structurally supports sustained pricing power and above-cost-of-capital returns. Map the borrower's position in its value chain — identify where margin is generated and captured, whether that node is structurally defensible, and whether competitive dynamics are stable or deteriorating. Distinguish between a business that is good because the industry is good versus one that is competitively differentiated within a mediocre industry. Flag structural industry deterioration regardless of near-term borrower performance.

**(2) Revenue decomposition — price, volume, and mix:** Disaggregate revenue changes into their component value factors: volume (unit growth), price realization (pricing power, contractual escalators, competitive pricing pressure), and mix (customer, product, or channel mix shift). Treat each independently. Volume growth that relies on market share gains in a mature industry implies competitive displacement — identify the source. Price growth that exceeds industry norms implies pricing power — stress-test its durability. Flag when management's revenue trajectory sits in the upper decile of its peer cohort by size and industry; build the base case and stress case anchored to the median of that distribution, not to management's inside view.

**(3) Operating leverage and cost structure:** Decompose the fixed vs. variable cost structure explicitly. The degree of operating leverage determines how revenue changes flow through to EBITDA — high fixed-cost businesses amplify both upside and downside. Identify the contribution margin profile and the revenue level at which fixed costs are covered, then stress revenue at and below that inflection point to quantify downside EBITDA behavior. Distinguish between cost efficiencies (permanent unit cost reductions from process, procurement, or technology improvement) and economies of scale (margin expansion that is volume-dependent and reverses in a downturn). The former is more durable; the latter must be explicitly stress-tested.

**(4) EBITDA quality and normalization:** Apply heightened skepticism to addbacks given the absence of sponsor pre-diligence. Interrogate one-time items, owner compensation normalization, pro forma adjustments, and the sustainability of margin levels against the base rate distribution for the borrower's industry and size cohort. Flag when LTM or adjusted EBITDA implies margins in the upper quartile of the peer distribution — mean reversion is the base case. Per Mauboussin's base rate data, sales growth, operating margins, and ROIC all exhibit strong mean reversion; above-cohort performance is not the default forward assumption.

**(5) Capital intensity — capex and NWC:** Assess maintenance vs. growth capex and capex intensity relative to revenue and depreciation. Flag understated capex through deferred maintenance, which is common in founder-owned businesses. Separately analyze net working capital intensity and the direction of NWC change — deteriorating receivables, inventory build, or shortening payables cycles consume cash even when EBITDA is growing. Model FCF as the primary debt service metric, not EBITDA.

**(6) Financial leverage and tax:** Model the full effect of debt service on FCF — interest coverage across rate scenarios given floating rate SOFR exposure, amortization requirements, and PIK toggle optionality if applicable. Note any material deferred tax liabilities, NOL assets, or tax structure considerations that drive a wedge between cash taxes and book taxes.

**(7) Ownership, management, and governance:** Evaluate ownership structure, management depth, key-man risk, and succession planning. For founder- and family-owned businesses, assess whether the owner is an operator, a capital allocator, or neither — and what stress-scenario behavior looks like. Is ownership likely to be an aligned lender partner in a workout, or a source of friction?

**(8) Covenant structure and lender protections:** Evaluate covenant headroom and structural protections with particular rigor given the absence of a sophisticated sponsor counterparty. Stress covenant compliance using the operating leverage and base rate framework above. Identify cure rights, equity cure mechanics, and early warning triggers.

**(9) Downside and recovery:** Build stress cases anchored to the base rate distribution for the borrower's cohort — not to management projections. Anchor the downside to the median or lower-quartile revenue outcome for the relevant size and industry reference class, then run that revenue shock through the operating leverage model to derive stressed EBITDA, stressed FCF, and implied coverage. Assess recovery through asset coverage, enterprise value stability, and structural position in the capital stack.

**(10) Origination context:** Note Wells Fargo relationship context and any origination channel considerations relevant to assessing borrower quality, deal motivation, and lender dynamics.

## First Principles Orientation

Credit analysis begins with understanding the micro drivers of free cash flow — not with a leverage multiple relative to market convention. The analytical anchors are Porter for industry structure and value chain positioning, and Mauboussin for company-level financial behavior: what are the value factors driving revenue (price, volume, mix)? What does the fixed/variable cost structure imply about downside FCF through the operating leverage mechanism? Where does this borrower sit in the base rate distribution of its industry and size cohort?

Forecasts implying persistent top-quartile performance should be treated with structural skepticism — Mauboussin's base rate data demonstrate strong mean reversion across sales growth, margins, and ROIC, and founder/family-owned businesses lack the sponsor discipline that sometimes justifies above-cohort assumptions. When stress-testing a thesis, push back hard and identify weaknesses — do not default to validating the inside view. The goal is to find the gap between what the deal assumes and what the base rate distribution says is probable.
