# Overland Credit Framework

This is the firm's first-principles analytical methodology for non-sponsored middle-market underwriting, surfaced as institutional knowledge because it is the **shared spine** three production skills embed: [[prompt-generator-skill]] injects it into generated credit prompts, [[sector-research-screener]] applies its industry layer, and [[posting-memo-automation]] applies its base-rate layer to D&A and considerations. It is documented here once so those skills can reference rather than restate it. The orientation is explicit: credit analysis begins with the micro drivers of free cash flow, not a leverage multiple relative to market convention — Porter for industry structure, Mauboussin for company-level financial behavior and base-rate discipline.

## Credit quality screen

A two-layer pass/flag screen applied before and alongside the FCF work.

**Industry-level.** Genuine secular tailwinds (demographic, regulatory, technological, infrastructure — distinguished from cyclical recoveries); acyclical or only modestly cyclical demand; and a fragmented competitive structure where the borrower is a larger regional/national provider competing against sub-regional and mom-and-pop operators, with credible strategic acquirers at scale as a "second way out."

**Company-level.** Demand-driver quality in strict priority order — contractual recurring > reoccurring (behavioral) > non-discretionary break-fix, with project-based/transactional/discretionary revenue flagged; concentration discipline (caution >10% of revenue, material risk >20–25% absent protection); a 2–3 year revenue/GP/EBITDA growth trend; target margins (gross 30–40%+, EBITDA 12–20%+) with mean-reversion skepticism applied *above* the upper end as well as below; limited capex/NWC intensity with FCF — not EBITDA — modeled as the debt-service metric; and a credit-accretive DDTL use case (a strong roll-up/expansion pipeline is a quality signal, an unanchored accordion a risk).

## FCF decomposition sequence

The thesis is built bottom-up through ten steps: (1) industry & competitive structure (Porter); (2) revenue decomposition into price/volume/mix; (3) operating leverage and fixed/variable cost structure, distinguishing durable cost efficiencies from volume-dependent scale economies; (4) EBITDA quality and addback normalization under heightened non-sponsored skepticism; (5) capital intensity (maintenance vs. growth capex, NWC direction); (6) financial leverage and the cash-vs-book-tax wedge; (7) ownership, management, key-man and workout behavior; (8) covenant headroom and lender protections; (9) downside and recovery anchored to the cohort base-rate distribution rather than management projections; (10) origination context. The recurring instruction across steps: when management's trajectory sits in the upper decile/quartile of its size-and-industry cohort, build the base and stress cases off the cohort median, because sales growth, margins, and ROIC all exhibit strong mean reversion.

## Base-rate evidence hierarchy

Any external benchmarking must be grounded in one of three evidence tiers, in priority order: **Tier 1** public comps with observable metrics; **Tier 2** user-provided base rates; **Tier 3** comp/precedent data embedded in the CIM/QoE/attached materials. Absent all three, the analysis **defaults to internal historical benchmarking** — measure current metrics against the fullest available company history (including recessionary periods), flag material step-changes, and frame strictly relative to the company's own history with no invented external distribution. Fabricating or implying industry-level base-rate statistics is prohibited; "base rate" / "mean reversion" framing and any named academic/practitioner author are barred from memo output even when the analysis is sound.

## Industry attractiveness screen

The sourcing-side projection of the same logic: eight Porter-derived FCF signals (high entry barriers, non-discretionary/mission-critical demand, fragmented low-power buyers with switching friction, low supplier power, low substitution risk, rational pricing, low capex intensity / high FCF conversion, secular non-GDP demand drivers) plus a "Goldilocks" semi-fragmented structure (national HHI ~600–1,500, local operator concentration). The **three-tier competitive structure** is the operative model: Tier 1 national consolidators (the second-way-out acquirers), Tier 2 regional incumbents ($5–50M EBITDA — the Overland borrower), and a Tier 3 owner-operator tail (the competitive set and the tuck-in pipeline). Local fragmentation is explicitly *not* pricing risk because switching friction, a service-reliability premium, compliance documentation, and relationship tenure suppress price-led churn.
